One ROI formula:

\[ \begin{aligned} n_{leads} &: \text{Number of leads} \\ p_c &: \text{Lead to customer conversion rate} \\ \bar{s} &:\text{Average sales price per customer} \\ ROI &:\frac{n_{leads} p_c \bar{s} - cost}{cost} \end{aligned} \]

  • ROI can be extended to include additional traffic, likes on social media, stronger brand (more trust, familarity, …)

Return on an a brand campaign based on impressions or reach, cost of the campaign, and the proportion that changed their attitude (\(p_{changed}\)):

\[ efficiency = \frac{Cost}{Reach*p_{changed}} \]

Retargeting: Ads to a user who previously visited but did not convert

  • Pixel-based retargeting: A Pixel refers to JavaScript code placed on a visitors browser (to “cookie” the browser). The cookie notifies retargeting platforms to serve specific ads. The advantage of pixel-based retargeting is that it is timely (they can be retargeted pretty immediately after leaving your site), specific to a particular page on your site, and behavior-based. Downsides to this method are that there is a lower volume of people in the campaign at any given moment in time since it’s all based on how often people are coming to your website, viewing certain pages, and leaving. It also can be complicated or time-intensive to implement the JavaScript on many website pages.
  • List-based retargeting: Upload a list of emails or other identifying information to an ad server (e.g. facebook), then target ads to only those people.

Article on common problems here:

  1. Not enough power
  • Small effect size
  • Results are different from those used to estimate effect size
  • Limited sample size because of external factors
  • Defined audience is too small – not enough people get to the assignment part of a userflow
  • Outcomes don’t happen frequently
  • Purchase cycle duration long relative to the time the experiment runs – future conversions aren’t observed
  1. Outliers
  • Generative model? Are the outliers natural or are they errors or anomolies?
  • Are they important for the decision being made – Is the decision wrt the population or the norm?
  • Solution: Winsorizing, or Wilcox rank test
  • Also, imputation with median or mean
  1. Counfounds; independent variables not isolated
  • Bids, budgets, creatives, … should all be controlled across conditions
  • Violation of stable units (a T influences a C)
  • Can ignore if the exposure might be small?
  • Can attempt to model out the exposure
  • Random events in only one condition, e.g. a geo split then a natural disaster happening in one of the arms
  • Can change the interpretation of the test to reflect the exposure
  1. Crossover (C exposed to T)
  • Are cookies being used?
  • Can happen from Unstable identification (e.g. cookies)
  • Network effects: people in one condition talk to those in another condition
  • Someone else access the account, e.g. in a household another member may order for the house from a partner’s amazon account
  • Usually dampen an estimation of an effect, so if an effect is significant it is usually trustworthy
  1. Engagement or novelty effects, or second-order effects
  • Ads can influence lifetime value beyond the target variable in the study
  • Ads can influence conversions on other products
  • Ads can influence conversions in a partisular, non-target channel (e.g. in store)
  • Ads can convert potential future customers, just sooner – i.e. not generate demand, but rather shift it forward in time.
  • Experimentors should monitor downstream outcomes as well
  • Careful choice of the outcome variable and experiment duration are valuable here
  1. Unmeasured important variables
  • E.g. retail cash transactions
  • Multiple unmatchable user IDs or emails
  • Users can use different devices and browsers
  • Survey responses might be low
  • If these issues are scarce (e.g. few cash transactions) and balanced (e.g. roughly equal proportion of cash users in T and C) then they can be ignored
